Areas of Research: Strategy, Organizational Behaviour

Brief Summary: Management and economics research has investigated how organizations can improve coordination in environments in which coordination is challenging (e.g., Van Huyck et al., 1990; Weber et al., 2001). Prior research has shown that increasing bonuses (Brandts and Cooper, 2006) and pre-play communication (Chen and Chen, 2011) can improve groups' ability to choose matching numbers. This study looks at how giving people the ability to chat before working impacts their actual ability to coordinate in a work task that requires participants to exert effort. The experiment you completed today is a pilot study that will be used to check whether participants understand the game, understand the purpose of the experiment, and can navigate the program easily. It is important to note that economics experiments always use incentives to approximate how organizations and markets encourage people to make decisions. Moreover, economics experiments also have a non-deception policy, so experimenters cannot 'lie' in describing the experimental tasks or goals. Psychology experiments are often unincentivized and sometimes involve deception.

Specific Hypothesis Tested (H1): Groups that have the ability to chat before playing their round will coordinate better than those who were not given the ability to chat.

Independent Variables: Group membership (chat v. no chat).

Dependent Variables: Individual sentences typed and minimum of group sentences typed.

Control procedures: Those who were told to write "long reflections" were in the control group, and those who were given the opportunity to chat were in the treatment group.

Implications for theory/practice: Understanding the reasons why individuals actually coordinate in groups will give us a better understanding into how firms build sustainable cultures that reduce general levels of strategic uncertainty in the workplace.
